Landmark Case Laws

Supreme Court decisions that shaped constitutional interpretation in India

1950

A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras

Issue: Validity of preventive detention under Article 22. Whether Article 21 requires procedure to be just, fair, and reasonable.

Judgment: Adopted a narrow interpretation of Article 21. "Procedure established by law" simply means procedure prescribed by law enacted by the legislature, not necessarily a just or fair procedure.

First major case on personal liberty. Later overruled by Maneka Gandhi case.
1951

State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan

Issue: Validity of communal reservation in educational institutions. Conflict between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles.

Judgment: Fundamental Rights prevail over Directive Principles. Led to the 1st Amendment adding Article 15(4) to allow special provisions for backward classes.

Established initial hierarchy between Fundamental Rights and DPSP.
1967

Golaknath v. State of Punjab

Issue: Whether Parliament has the power to amend Fundamental Rights.

Judgment: Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights as they are "transcendental and immutable." The amending power under Article 368 is not unlimited.

Landmark — later overruled by 24th Amendment and Kesavananda Bharati.
1973

Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala ⭐

Issue: Whether Parliament has unlimited power to amend the Constitution, including Fundamental Rights.

Judgment: Established the Basic Structure Doctrine. Parliament can amend any provision but cannot alter the "basic structure" of the Constitution (e.g., supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic government, secular character, separation of powers, federal character).

The most significant constitutional case. Decided by the largest ever bench of 13 judges.
1978

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India ⭐

Issue: Whether the right to travel abroad is part of "personal liberty" under Article 21. Whether procedure must be "fair, just, and reasonable."

Judgment: Revolutionized Article 21. Right to life and personal liberty includes a wide range of rights. Procedure under Article 21 must be "fair, just, and reasonable" (not merely any law). Overruled the narrow interpretation of A.K. Gopalan.

Expanded Article 21 to include right to dignity, livelihood, education, health, travel.
1980

Minerva Mills v. Union of India ⭐

Issue: Validity of 42nd Amendment provisions that gave DPSP primacy over all Fundamental Rights and made constitutional amendments non-reviewable.

Judgment: Struck down the 42nd Amendment provisions. Reaffirmed the Basic Structure Doctrine. The balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles is itself a basic feature that cannot be destroyed.

Reinforced judicial review as a basic feature of the Constitution.
1985

MC Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak)

Issue: Industrial disaster causing harm. Scope of liability for hazardous industries.

Judgment: Established the principle of "Absolute Liability" — any enterprise engaged in inherently dangerous activity is absolutely liable for compensation, regardless of precautions taken. Goes beyond "strict liability" (Rylands v. Fletcher).

Pioneering environmental law jurisprudence in India.
1992

Indra Sawhney v. Union of India ⭐

Issue: Validity of 27% reservation for OBCs based on Mandal Commission recommendations.

Judgment: Upheld 27% OBC reservation but capped total reservation at 50%. Introduced the "creamy layer" concept — economically advanced persons among OBCs to be excluded from reservation. Reservation in promotions was rejected (later enabled by 77th Amendment).

Shaped India's reservation policy. The 50% cap remains the benchmark.
1994

S.R. Bommai v. Union of India ⭐

Issue: Scope and limits of Article 356 (President's Rule).

Judgment: Made President's Rule subject to judicial review. Secularism is a basic feature. Floor test is the standard to determine majority. The proclamation can be struck down if found mala fide or based on irrelevant considerations.

Curtailed misuse of Article 356. Strengthened federalism and secularism.
1997

Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan ⭐

Issue: Absence of legislation on sexual harassment at the workplace.

Judgment: Laid down comprehensive guidelines to prevent sexual harassment at the workplace. These were binding until Parliament enacted legislation. Led to the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 (POSH Act).

Landmark for women's rights. Court acted in the absence of legislation.
2017

K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India ⭐

Issue: Whether Right to Privacy is a fundamental right under the Constitution.

Judgment: Unanimously declared Right to Privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21. Overruled M.P. Sharma (1954) and Kharak Singh (1962). Privacy covers informational privacy, bodily autonomy, and decisional autonomy.

Nine-judge bench unanimous decision. Foundation for data protection laws.
2018

Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India ⭐

Issue: Constitutional validity of Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code criminalizing consensual homosexual acts.

Judgment: Decriminalized consensual homosexual acts by partially striking down Section 377 as unconstitutional. Violated Articles 14 (equality), 15 (non-discrimination), 19 (freedom of expression), and 21 (life and dignity).

Historic for LGBTQ+ rights. Upheld individual liberty and dignity.
