Articles 1–395

Browse every Article of the Indian Constitution organized by Parts

Art. 1 Name and territory of the Union▼
Part I

India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States. The territory comprises: (a) territories of the States, (b) Union territories, (c) territories that may be acquired.

Art. 2 Admission or establishment of new States▼
Part I

Parliament may by law admit into the Union, or establish, new States on such terms and conditions as it thinks fit.

Art. 3 Formation of new States and alteration of boundaries▼
Part I

Parliament may by law form a new State by separation of territory from any State, or by uniting two or more States. It may increase or diminish the area of any State or alter its boundaries or name.

Art. 4 Laws made under Articles 2 and 3▼
Part I

Any law referred to in Articles 2 or 3 shall contain such provisions for amending the First and Fourth Schedules as may be necessary.

Art. 5 Citizenship at the commencement of the Constitution▼
Part II

At the commencement of the Constitution, every person who has his domicile in India and was born in India, or either of whose parents was born in India, or has been ordinarily resident in India for not less than five years, shall be a citizen of India.

Art. 6 Rights of citizenship of persons who have migrated to India from Pakistan▼
Part II

Deals with the rights of citizenship of persons who migrated from Pakistan to India before and after July 19, 1948.

Art. 7–11 Other citizenship provisions▼
Part II

Articles 7–11 deal with: rights of citizenship of persons migrated to Pakistan (7), persons of Indian origin residing outside India (8), not to be citizen if voluntarily acquired citizenship of a foreign State (9), continuance of rights of citizenship (10), and Parliament's power to regulate the right of citizenship by law (11).

Art. 12 Definition of State▼
Part III

The State includes the Government and Parliament of India, the Government and Legislature of each of the States, and all local or other authorities within or under the control of the Government of India.

Art. 13 Laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights▼
Part III

All laws in force before the Constitution, in so far as they are inconsistent with Part III, shall be void. The State shall not make any law which takes away or abridges the rights conferred by Part III.

Art. 14 Equality before law▼
Part III

The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India. This is based on the English concept of Rule of Law and the American concept of Equal Protection.

Art. 15 Prohibition of discrimination▼
Part III

The State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth or any of them. However, special provisions can be made for women, children, socially and educationally backward classes, SCs, and STs.

Art. 16 Equality of opportunity in public employment▼
Part III

There shall be equality of opportunity for all citizens in matters relating to employment or appointment to any office under the State. However, reservation can be made for any backward class of citizens.

Art. 17 Abolition of Untouchability▼
Part III

"Untouchability" is abolished and its practice in any form is forbidden. The enforcement of any disability arising out of "Untouchability" shall be an offence punishable in accordance with law.

Art. 18 Abolition of titles▼
Part III

No title (not being a military or academic distinction) shall be conferred by the State. No citizen of India shall accept any title from any foreign State.

Art. 19 Protection of six freedoms▼
Part III

All citizens shall have the right to: (a) freedom of speech and expression; (b) assemble peaceably and without arms; (c) form associations or unions; (d) move freely throughout India; (e) reside and settle in any part of India; (g) practise any profession, or carry on any occupation, trade or business. (Right (f) was repealed by 44th Amendment)

Art. 20 Protection against conviction for offences▼
Part III

No person shall be: (1) convicted for an act that was not an offence when committed (no ex-post facto law); (2) punished more than once for the same offence (no double jeopardy); (3) compelled to be a witness against himself (no self-incrimination).

Art. 21 Protection of life and personal liberty▼
Part III

No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law. This is one of the most important articles and has been expanded to include the right to live with dignity, right to livelihood, right to education, right to health, and many more.

Art. 21A Right to Education▼
Part III

The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of 6 to 14 years. Added by the 86th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2002.

Art. 22 Protection against arrest and detention▼
Part III

Every arrested person has the right to: (1) be informed of grounds of arrest; (2) consult and be defended by a legal practitioner; (3) be produced before a magistrate within 24 hours. Also contains provisions for preventive detention.

Art. 23 Prohibition of forced labour▼
Part III

Traffic in human beings and begar (forced labour) and other similar forms of forced labour are prohibited. Any contravention is an offence punishable by law.

Art. 24 Prohibition of child labour▼
Part III

No child below the age of fourteen years shall be employed to work in any factory or mine or engaged in any other hazardous employment.

Art. 25–28 Freedom of Religion▼
Part III

Articles 25–28 guarantee: freedom of conscience and right to profess, practise, and propagate religion (25); freedom to manage religious affairs (26); freedom from paying taxes for promotion of any religion (27); freedom from attending religious instruction in certain institutions (28).

Art. 29–30 Cultural and Educational Rights▼
Part III

Article 29: Protection of interests of minorities — right to conserve distinct language, script, or culture. Article 30: Right of minorities to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice.

Art. 32 Right to Constitutional Remedies▼
Part III

The right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights is guaranteed. The Supreme Court can issue writs: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Certiorari, and Quo Warranto. Dr. Ambedkar called this Article the "heart and soul" of the Constitution.

Art. 36–51 Directive Principles of State Policy▼
Part IV

Articles 36–51 contain the Directive Principles. These are non-justiciable guidelines for the State to frame laws. They aim to establish social and economic democracy. Key articles include: Article 39 (principles of policy), Article 40 (village panchayats), Article 44 (uniform civil code), Article 45 (early childhood education), Article 48A (environment protection). See detailed DPSP page →

Art. 51A Fundamental Duties▼
Part IVA

Lists 11 Fundamental Duties of citizens including: respect for the Constitution, national flag and anthem; cherish ideals of freedom struggle; uphold sovereignty and integrity of India; defend the country; promote harmony; preserve rich heritage; protect environment; develop scientific temper; safeguard public property; strive for excellence; provide education for children aged 6–14. See detailed Duties page →

Art. 52–78 The Executive (President, VP, Council of Ministers)▼
Part V

Articles 52–78 deal with the Union Executive: The President (52–62), Vice-President (63–71), Council of Ministers (74–75), and Attorney-General (76). Key provisions: President is the head of state (52), executive power vested in President (53), election of President (54), impeachment (61), power to grant pardons (72), Council of Ministers headed by PM to aid and advise President (74).

Art. 79–122 Parliament▼
Part V

Articles 79–122 deal with Parliament, which consists of the President and two Houses — Rajya Sabha (Council of States) and Lok Sabha (House of the People). Key provisions: composition of Houses (80–81), qualifications for membership (84), sessions (85), Speaker and Deputy Speaker (93), legislative procedure (107–111), Money Bills (110), Budget (112), Ordinance-making power (123).

Art. 124–147 The Supreme Court▼
Part V

Articles 124–147 deal with the Supreme Court: establishment and constitution (124), appointment and qualification of judges (124), salaries (125), original jurisdiction (131), appellate jurisdiction (132–134), special leave to appeal (136), review of judgments (137), Supreme Court as court of record (129), advisory jurisdiction (143).

Art. 148–151 Comptroller and Auditor-General of India▼
Part V

The CAG is appointed by the President and can only be removed like a Supreme Court Judge. The CAG audits all receipts and expenditures of the Union and States and reports to the President/Governor.

Art. 152–237 The States (Governor, Legislature, High Courts)▼
Part VI

Part VI is the state-level counterpart of Part V. It covers: Governor (153–167), Council of Ministers (163–164), State Legislature (168–212), High Courts (214–231), and Subordinate Courts (233–237). Key provisions include: Governor as head of state (153), Council of Ministers headed by CM (163), Legislative procedure, High Court writs (226).

Art. 238 States in Part B of the First Schedule (Repealed)▼
Part VII

Part VII originally dealt with States in Part B of the First Schedule. It was repealed by the 7th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1956.

Art. 239–242 Union Territories▼
Part VIII

Deals with administration of Union Territories. The President administers UTs through an administrator appointed by him. Some UTs (like Delhi and Puducherry) have their own legislatures.

Art. 243–243O The Panchayats▼
Part IX

Added by the 73rd Amendment (1992). Provides constitutional status to Panchayati Raj institutions. Three-tier system: Gram Panchayat (village), Panchayat Samiti (block), Zila Parishad (district). Reservation for SCs, STs, and women. Five-year term. State Election Commission for elections.

Art. 243P–243ZG The Municipalities▼
Part IXA

Added by the 74th Amendment (1992). Provides constitutional status to urban local bodies: Nagar Panchayat (transitional area), Municipal Council (smaller urban area), Municipal Corporation (larger urban area). Reservation for SCs, STs, and women.

Art. 243ZH–243ZT Co-operative Societies▼
Part IXB

Added by the 97th Amendment (2011). Deals with co-operative societies, providing for their democratic functioning and autonomy. Ensures right to form co-operative societies, free and fair elections, and regular audit.

Art. 244–244A Scheduled and Tribal Areas▼
Part X

Deals with the administration of Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes under the Fifth and Sixth Schedules. Article 244A was added to allow formation of autonomous states within Assam.

Art. 245–263 Relations between the Union and the States▼
Part XI

Deals with legislative and administrative relations between Union and States. Key articles: extent of laws by Parliament and State Legislatures (245), Union/State/Concurrent Lists in Seventh Schedule (246), residuary powers of legislation (248), inconsistency between Union and State laws (254), Inter-State Council (263).

Art. 264–300A Finance, Property, Contracts, and Suits▼
Part XII

Deals with distribution of revenues between Union and States, Finance Commission (280), Consolidated Fund (266), Contingency Fund (267), borrowing (292–293), and right to property (300A – no person shall be deprived of property save by authority of law).

Art. 301–307 Trade, Commerce and Intercourse▼
Part XIII

Subject to other provisions of the Constitution, trade, commerce and intercourse throughout the territory of India shall be free. Parliament may impose restrictions in the public interest. No State can give preference to one state over another in trade matters.

Art. 308–323 Services under the Union and the States▼
Part XIV

Deals with civil services and Public Service Commissions. Key provisions: UPSC (315–323), State PSCs, safeguards for civil servants (311), All-India Services (312). Article 311 provides protection to civil servants from arbitrary dismissal.

Art. 323A–323B Tribunals▼
Part XIVA

Added by the 42nd Amendment. Article 323A empowers Parliament to establish Administrative Tribunals. Article 323B empowers Parliament and State Legislatures to establish tribunals for other matters like taxation, foreign exchange, industrial disputes, etc.

Art. 324–329A Elections▼
Part XV

Deals with elections. Key provisions: Election Commission of India (324), adult suffrage (326), no person to be ineligible for electoral roll on grounds of religion, race, caste or sex (325). The Election Commission supervises all elections to Parliament and State Legislatures.

Art. 330–342 Special Provisions for Certain Classes▼
Part XVI

Deals with reservation of seats for SCs and STs in Lok Sabha (330) and State Assemblies (332), Anglo-Indian representation (331, 333), National Commission for SCs (338) and STs (338A), reservation in services (335).

Art. 343–351 Official Language▼
Part XVII

Hindi in Devanagari script shall be the official language of the Union (343). English shall continue to be used for official purposes (343(2)). Regional languages are recognized in the Eighth Schedule. The Official Language Commission and Committee of Parliament on Official Language are provided for.

Art. 352–360 Emergency Provisions▼
Part XVIII

Three types of emergency: (1) National Emergency due to war, external aggression or armed rebellion (352); (2) President's Rule — failure of constitutional machinery in a State (356); (3) Financial Emergency (360). During National Emergency, Fundamental Rights (except Articles 20 and 21) can be suspended.

Art. 361–367 Miscellaneous▼
Part XIX

Deals with protection of President and Governors (361), rights of members of Parliament and State Legislatures (361A), bar of interference by courts in proceedings of Parliament (361B), and interpretation of the Constitution (367).

Art. 368 Amendment of the Constitution▼
Part XX

Parliament may amend the Constitution by way of addition, variation or repeal of any provision. Three types of amendments: (1) by simple majority; (2) by special majority (2/3 of members present and voting); (3) by special majority + ratification by half the State Legislatures. The basic structure of the Constitution cannot be amended (Kesavananda Bharati case).

Art. 369–392 Temporary, Transitional and Special Provisions▼
Part XXI

Contains temporary and special provisions including: Article 370 (special provisions for J&K — abrogated in 2019), Article 371–371J (special provisions for various states like Maharashtra, Gujarat, Nagaland, Assam, Manipur, Andhra Pradesh, Sikkim, Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, Karnataka, and Hyderabad-Karnataka).

Art. 393–395 Short title, Commencement, Repeals▼
Part XXII

Article 393: This Constitution may be called the Constitution of India. Article 394: Articles 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 60, 324, 366, 367, 379, 380, 388, 391, 392 and 393 came into force on 26 November 1949; the remaining provisions came into force on 26 January 1950. Article 395: The Indian Independence Act, 1947, and the Government of India Act, 1935 are repealed.
